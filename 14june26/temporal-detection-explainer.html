Detecting coherent backdoor clients across rounds — under privacy, and apart from the heterogeneity barrier

A plain walkthrough of the idea, the terms, and the results — written to be honest about what I can prove, what I can only derive, what I have only checked in simulation, and what is still open.

This note summarises the workshop paper “A Spiked-Covariance Threshold for Sequential Detection of Coherent Byzantine Blocks, Orthogonal to the Heterogeneity Barrier.” I have kept the equations to the statements only — no proofs — and put the emphasis on why each object is introduced and what each result means.

1 The problem

Stated in words; no notation.

In federated learning a central server trains a shared model with the help of many clients. Each round, every client computes an update on its own private data and sends it in; the server combines the updates and takes a step. Two facts make this hard to secure at once:

  • Honest clients legitimately disagree. Their local data differ, so even with no attacker their updates point in somewhat different directions. This spread is called heterogeneity, and it is unavoidable.
  • Some clients may be adversarial, and they can coordinate.

The threat I focus on is not a client that sends obvious garbage — that is easy to clip or discard each round. It is a coordinated subset of clients that nudges the model a little, in one shared malicious direction, every round. Each single nudge is small enough to look like ordinary disagreement, so any per-round defence waves it through; but the nudges all point the same way, so over many rounds they accumulate into a planted “backdoor.” This is the realistic targeted attack: quiet per round, damaging over time.

There is one more requirement, and it is central rather than incidental: privacy. The server should never see any individual client’s update — only aggregates (sums over groups of clients). This is what “secure aggregation” means, and it is what real deployments want.

The question
Can the server figure out which clients are the coordinated adversaries — across rounds, and without ever seeing any one of them individually — given that honest clients already disagree among themselves?

2 What is already known

Stated in words; no notation. Each line below gives something — and stops short of something.

Line of workWhat it givesWhere it stops
Robust aggregation (median, trimmed mean, and relatives) Survives a minority of bad updates each round. It is memoryless — it discards the cross-round pattern — and it has a proven worst-case floor: when honest clients are heterogeneous, no aggregator can beat an error that grows with (bad fraction × heterogeneity).
A companion “barrier” result (my earlier note) Proves that no probability-based scoring layer can beat that floor against an attacker that perfectly imitates honest behaviour at every round. It is scoped to a persistent attacker (one that looks the same every round). It says nothing about an attacker that changes over time.
Distributed detection / sensor fusion Beautiful results on how fast detection error shrinks as evidence grows. For a single global yes/no question over identical sensors — never “which client,” and not under heterogeneity.
Group testing under secure aggregation Identifies culprits from pooled measurements, and has been brought into federated learning. Single-round, with error rates reported numerically — no guarantee about accumulating evidence across rounds.
Spiked-covariance / random-matrix theory A classical, sharp account of when a faint shared direction can be recovered from noisy high-dimensional data. A general statistical tool — not, on its own, a statement about federated learning. I use it; I do not extend it.

3 The gap I am trying to fill

Stated in words; no notation.

Put the wish-list in one place. I would like a guarantee that is all four of these at once:

  • per-client — it names which client, with a two-sided error guarantee (both “missed a real attacker” and “falsely accused an honest client”);
  • for federated-learning aggregation — not a generic sensor model;
  • honest about heterogeneity — it must not quietly assume honest clients agree;
  • against the realistic adversary — the time-varying coordinated backdoor, not a strawman.

No single line of prior work is all four. And the barrier seems to slam the door — until one notices exactly where it applies.

The opening
The barrier’s obstruction only bites when the attacker looks identical every round. A time-varying coordinated attacker is precisely the case the barrier leaves untouched — and it is the realistic one, since a static attack is both weaker and easier to clip. So the door the barrier closes is for the persistent attacker; for the time-varying one it stands open.
The reframe — what makes detection possible
Honest disagreement is idiosyncratic and independent: each client drifts its own way, for its own data reasons. The attack is the opposite — shared and structured: the same direction, reused round after round. That shared structure is a fingerprint that honest noise does not have. The attack’s own shared, reused structure across rounds is what betrays it.

4 What has to be shown

The mathematical obligations, in words — no notation yet. These are the promises the formal results then keep.

  1. Evidence accumulates. Show that against this time-varying attacker, more rounds genuinely help — and pin down how many rounds are needed before identification is even possible.
  2. A per-client guarantee. Show a two-sided bound on the chance of missing a real attacker or falsely flagging an honest client — and that it improves with rounds.
  3. Optimal, and no flood of false accusations. Show the test is the best one the data allow, and that false alarms vanish as rounds grow.
  4. Independent of heterogeneity. Show the detector’s power does not depend on the heterogeneity that defeats robust aggregation — i.e. it lives on a different axis than the barrier.
  5. Consistency with the barrier. Show that when the attacker stops varying, the method gracefully reproduces the known floor — recovering prior theory rather than contradicting it.
  6. Still works under privacy. Show the whole pipeline runs from group sums alone, never seeing individuals — and quantify what privacy costs (more rounds, or coarser localization).

5 The pieces, and why each is needed

A handful of objects do all the work. For each I give what it is and — the part that matters — what would break without it.

The dials

A round index $t=1,\dots,T$; $n$ clients; updates living in $d$ dimensions; a fraction $\delta$ of clients adversarial; heterogeneity $\zeta$ (how much honest clients differ); per-round noise $\sigma$.

The shared target direction $v$

A single fixed direction the whole adversarial block pushes toward, every round.

Why we need it: a coherent backdoor has to steer the model toward some specific target and keep steering there. That reuse of one direction is the only thing that structurally separates the attack from honest noise — so $v$ is the thing the detector ultimately hunts for.

The non-stationary residual $r_i^t$ — the keystone

Before looking at anything, subtract two things from each client’s update: (i) the per-round consensus everyone shares, and (ii) that client’s own average over time.

$$ r_i^t \;=\; \big(g_i^t - \hat c_t\big)\;-\;\tfrac{1}{T}\sum_{s\le T}\big(g_i^s - \hat c_s\big) $$

Why we need it: subtracting each client’s own running average deletes its constant tilt — and a client’s heterogeneity is exactly such a constant tilt. It also deletes any constant part of an attack. What is left is only the fluctuation. This single step is why the heterogeneity that blocks robust aggregation simply cancels out here instead of being mistaken for an attacker.

Under privacy: the server cannot do this per client — but it does not need to. The identical subtraction runs one level up, on group sums, because averaging commutes with summing: a group’s time-mean is the sum of its members’ time-means, so the constant per-client tilts still cancel without any individual update ever being seen. (This is why the detection groups are held fixed across rounds, and why pinning blame to a single client then needs overlapping groups — see Proposition 7.)

The energy along $v$ — a variance signal, not a coherence one

Measure how much each client’s leftover fluctuation lines up with the target direction, and how strong it is.

Why this and not something else: once heterogeneity is removed as an average effect, the attacker still leaves a trace — extra variance along $v$. A variance signal is a second-moment effect, and heterogeneity (an average effect) cannot imitate it. We deliberately use this rather than cross-client coherence, because a clever attacker can desynchronise the timing of its nudges, which destroys coherence — but the variance trace survives desynchronisation.

The signature is the attack’s time-variation: the constant part of the push is removed alongside heterogeneity, so a perfectly constant attack leaves no trace — the undetectable boundary ($\Var(a)\to0$, Corollary 1). There a constant within-cloud push is statistically indistinguishable from honest data skew, and is bounded by the robust aggregator instead — so nothing is lost by not detecting it.

The estimated direction $\vhat$ (a “spike”)

The single direction along which the pooled leftover fluctuations are unusually energetic — found as the top eigenvector of their covariance.

Why we need it: the true $v$ is the attacker’s secret. So we estimate it. Pooling everyone’s residuals turns the attack into one outlier direction (a “spike”) sticking out of otherwise shapeless noise; $\vhat$ is our read of it.

The threshold $T^\star$

The number of rounds beyond which the spike can be recovered at all.

Why we need it: recovering a faint direction in many dimensions from noisy data has a sharp feasibility frontier — below it the estimate is pure noise, above it it locks onto the truth. $T^\star$ is the answer to “how many rounds,” and it is the headline number.

The consensus error $\tau^2$

The little extra noise introduced because, in practice, the per-round consensus is estimated (robustly), not known exactly.

Why we need it: heterogeneity was supposed to cancel — but estimating the consensus is the one place it can sneak back in. $\tau^2$ is that one door, so the whole “independent of heterogeneity” claim rests on showing $\tau^2$ stays small.

The generative model, for reference

An honest client and a block member differ by one term — the shared push $a_t^j v$, with a time-varying strength $a_t^j$:

$$ \text{honest } i:\ \ g_i^t = c_t + \mu_i + \xi_i^t \qquad\qquad \text{block } j:\ \ g_j^t = c_t + \mu_j + a_t^j\,v + \varepsilon_j^t $$

$c_t$ is what everyone agrees on that round; $\mu_i$ is a client’s persistent tilt (its heterogeneity); $\xi,\varepsilon$ are fresh per-round noise — the same distribution $\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$ for both, written with two symbols only to mark honest vs. block; making the block’s noise identical to honest noise is deliberate, so the shared push $a_t^j v$ is the only thing that gives a block member away. The strongest version of the attack lets each member vary $a_t^j$ on its own schedule (only $v$ is shared) — and that is the case the analysis targets.

6 The method, end to end

Here is the actual pipeline, concretely — from raw client updates to a per-client suspicion score. This is the private (secure-aggregation) version; the transparent version is the same with the grouping and secure-sum layers stripped out. Two things are easy to get wrong, so up front: the detection groups are fixed across rounds (not re-drawn each round), and secure aggregation here means summation.

client updates→ fixed overlapping groups→ secure sum $G_{t,g}$→ remove $c_t$→ group time-centering→ top eigenvector $\hat v$→ per-group energy→ decode $A^\top y$→ per-client score→ AUC
  1. Inputs. Each round $t$, the $n$ clients submit $d$-dimensional updates $g_i^t$. (In the experiments these are synthetic gradient streams generated with the honest/block structure — there is no trained toy model; that isolates the detection mechanism. The pipeline applies unchanged to real federated gradients.)
  2. Grouping — a fixed, overlapping design. Fix a design $A$ once: $m$ groups, each of size $s$, each client in $L$ groups (overlap). It is held fixed across all rounds — not re-randomised per round — because the later steps rely on each group’s membership being constant in time. (The experiments use a random overlapping design; a deployment would use a structured code with a target privacy level $r$. This is the detection channel of a two-channel design — the ordinary learning channel may still re-bucket freshly each round.)
  3. Secure aggregation — group sums. An ideal additive-masking primitive reveals only the group sums $G_{t,g}=\sum_{i\in S_g} g_i^t$ — sums, never individuals (averaging is just $G_{t,g}/s$, identical for equal known sizes). Privacy: the server cannot isolate fewer than $r$ clients.
  4. Strip the shared signal $c_t$ (each round). Subtract the common gradient. From group sums alone the global mean $\tfrac1n\sum_i g_i^t=\sum_g G_{t,g}/\sum_g s_g$ is computable and, under the desync threat, heterogeneity-free. (This is the one place a robust median can enter: in the transparent version $\hat c_t=\mathrm{median}_i\, g_i^t$ across clients. It only estimates the signal to subtract — it does not produce the model — and it is what injects the small $\tau^2$ noise of §5 / Proposition 5.)
  5. Per-group time-centering. Subtract each group’s own average over the $T$ rounds. This deletes the constant-in-time summed heterogeneity $\sum_{i\in S_g}\mu_i$ and any constant attack, leaving the fluctuation residual $R_{t,g}$. (The keystone of §5, done at group level — and exactly why the groups had to be fixed.)
  6. Recover the attack direction $\hat v$ — Stage 1. Pool the residual covariance $\hat C=\tfrac{1}{Tm}\sum_{t,g}R_{t,g}R_{t,g}^\top$ and take its top eigenvector. The rank-one $vv^\top$ spike survives summation, so $\hat v$ is recoverable from group sums once $T$ exceeds $T^\star$ (Theorem 1, Proposition 7).
  7. Per-group energy, then decode to clients — Stage 2. Per group, $E_g=\tfrac1T\sum_t\langle R_{t,g},\hat v\rangle^2$; subtract a robust baseline $y_g=E_g-\mathrm{median}_{g'} E_{g'}$ (the second, minor role of “median”). Back-project to per-client scores by the group-testing decode $\mathrm{score}=A^\top y$: a client scores high if it sits in many high-energy groups, and the overlap ($L\ge2$) gives each client a distinct group-signature that localises the sum to individuals — without ever seeing one.
  8. The probabilistic decision — what “probabilistic overlay” means here. In the clean per-client view each score is the energy $s_i$ along $\hat v$, a $\chi^2$ scale (variance) statistic. Deciding “block or honest” is a per-client test: thresholding $s_i$ is the likelihood-ratio / UMP test (Proposition 3), and its false-alarm / missed-detection probabilities carry the closed-form Bhattacharyya bound (Proposition 2). So the “probabilistic” content is the calibrated per-client error probabilities — a frequentist scale test, not a Bayesian posterior (the method emits a score plus provable error rates, not a literal “probability $\pi_i$ of being malicious”).
  9. Evaluation — AUC of the scores. Rank clients by their per-client score, compare to the true block labels, and report $1-\mathrm{AUC}$ (0 = perfect ranking, 0.5 = chance). That is the number on every experiment axis — AUC of the detection scores, not of posteriors.
Transparent version, and the parallel learning channel

Without privacy the detector is steps 4–9 run directly on the per-client residuals $r_i^t$ — no grouping, no decode — scoring each client straight from its own energy along $\hat v$. Meanwhile a standard robust aggregator (median, geometric-median / RFA, …) runs in parallel on the learning channel to produce the model and bound each round’s damage; the detector consumes the same updates but builds the cross-round variance evidence the aggregator discards.

7 The results, stated plainly

Statements only — no proofs. For each: what it defines, what it claims, and why we needed it. The status tag on each is deliberate.

One reduction underlies everything. After the residual step, pooling the leftover fluctuations across all clients and rounds gives a covariance that is flat noise plus a single bump along the attacker’s direction:

$$ \widehat C=\frac1N\sum_{i,t} r_i^t (r_i^t)^\top,\qquad \E[\widehat C]\;\approx\;\sigma_\eff^2\,I_d \;+\; \delta\,\Var(a)\,vv^\top . $$

Here $\sigma_\eff^2=\sigma^2+\tau^2$ is the effective noise floor. Recovering that one bump is a textbook problem (“spiked covariance”), and that is the bridge to the classical theory.

Theorem 1 — the detection threshold derived per-client bound open
$$ T^\star \;=\; \frac{d\,\sigma_\eff^4}{n\,\delta^2\,\Var(a)^2} $$

Reads as: below $T^\star$ rounds the attacker’s direction is unrecoverable (the estimate is random); above it, it locks on, and the per-client scores separate attackers from honest clients. The formula also says which way each dial pushes: more dimensions $d$ → harder; more attackers $\delta$ or a stronger fluctuation $\Var(a)$ → easier; more clients $n$ → easier.

Why we needed it: it answers obligation 1 — it makes “evidence accumulates” quantitative by naming the number of rounds.

Honest status. This is a derivation by reduction to the classical spiked-covariance phase transition, not a self-contained from-scratch proof. The direction-recovery frontier is classical; what is open is turning it into a rigorous two-sided per-client error bound, and the exact constant in $T^\star$ (the asymptotic constant is 1; the finite-size onset is a soft window). empirical The scaling is confirmed: on the clean direction-recovery marker the $\delta$-exponent is $\approx-2$ at every system size tried, and the $d,n,\Var(a)$ exponents match too. (A coarser early check looked shallower — that turned out to be a measurement artifact, not a wrong exponent.)

Proposition 2 — the per-client error floor proved

Once the direction is recovered, each client’s decision is a one-dimensional “is the variance elevated?” test. Its best-possible two-sided error is sandwiched by a single quantity $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}$ (a Bhattacharyya coefficient), which shrinks geometrically as rounds grow:

$$ \tfrac12\,\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}^2 \;\le\; P_{\mathrm{FA}}^{(i)}+P_{\mathrm{MD}}^{(i)} \;\le\; \rho_{\mathrm{BC}}, \qquad \rho_{\mathrm{BC}}=\Big(\tfrac{2\sqrt g}{1+g}\Big)^{T/2},\ \ g=1+\omega\beta . $$
Unpack it — what’s tested, what $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}$ is, and why the error collapses

What’s tested, and of what. The detector, not the aggregator: per client it asks “block member or honest?”. A false positive $P_{\mathrm{FA}}$ flags an honest client; a false negative $P_{\mathrm{MD}}$ misses a real attacker. (The aggregator’s own error is the separate $\Omega(\delta\zeta^2)$ floor — a different quantity.)

Two overlapping curves. The score $s_i$ — the energy of a client’s leftover wiggle along $\vhat$ — is a scaled $\chi^2_T$, centered at $\theta_0=\sigma_\eff^2$ for honest clients and higher at $\theta_1=\sigma_\eff^2+\omega\Var(a)$ for block members. Same shape, larger scale: the decision is a variance test between two overlapping distributions.

The Bhattacharyya coefficient. $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}\in[0,1]$ measures that overlap — geometrically, the cosine of the angle between $\sqrt{p_0}$ and $\sqrt{p_1}$. $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}=1$: identical, hopeless; $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}=0$: disjoint, trivial.

Why it collapses geometrically. Overlap is multiplicative over independent rounds: $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}=(\text{one-round overlap})^{T/2}$ — a factor below one, compounded round after round, because independent evidence multiplies. For a faint signal $\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}\approx e^{-T(\omega\beta)^2/16}$, i.e. error exponent $(\omega\beta)^2/16$. Below $T^\star$ the direction isn’t found ($\omega=0$, no separation, chance); above it the decay switches on. ($\omega$ = how well $\vhat$ found $v$; $\beta=\Var(a)/\sigma_\eff^2$ = attack-to-noise.)

What the inequality says. $P_{\mathrm{FA}}+P_{\mathrm{MD}}$ is the best possible total error. The upper bound $\le\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}$ is achievability (“at least this good”); the lower bound $\ge\tfrac12\rho_{\mathrm{BC}}^2$ is the floor / converse (“no test does better”). Both collapse geometrically, so together they characterize the error rather than merely bound it — and since Proposition 3 proves the energy test is optimal, the sandwich applies to the detector we actually run.

Why we needed it: this is obligation 2, and it is the per-client guarantee the federated-learning literature did not have — a closed-form floor and ceiling on the best achievable per-client error.

Propositions 3 & 4 — optimal, and false alarms vanish proved

Optimal: the energy score is the likelihood-ratio test — you cannot do better with the same data (within the natural family). False alarms collapse: with a fixed margin, the chance of falsely accusing an honest client decays exponentially in the number of rounds, and at a rate that does not depend on heterogeneity at all:

$$ P_{\mathrm{FA}}^{(i)}\;\le\; \exp\!\big(-T\,I(\kappa)\big), \qquad I(\kappa)=\tfrac12\big(\kappa-\log(1+\kappa)\big)\;>\;0 . $$

Why we needed it: obligation 3. A guarantee is only meaningful if (a) you are using the best test — otherwise some other test could beat the bound — and (b) buying detection power does not cost a flood of false accusations.

Proposition 5 & Corollary 2 — independence from heterogeneity proved (leading order)

Heterogeneity $\zeta$ enters the whole problem through exactly one channel — the consensus error $\tau^2$ — and that channel is small for every level of heterogeneity:

$$ \tau^2 \;=\; \frac{\zeta^2+\sigma^2}{n}\,\arccos\!\Big(\frac{\zeta^2}{\zeta^2+\sigma^2}\Big) \;=\; O(1/n). $$

So with an idealized consensus the detector’s power is flat in heterogeneity; the only $\zeta$-dependence in practice is the finite-sample price of estimating the consensus, and it fades as the number of clients grows.

Why we needed it: obligation 4 — this is what makes the method live on an axis orthogonal to the barrier. The barrier gates an averaging problem; this is a variance problem the averaging confound cannot reach.

Status. The formula is the leading-order (many-client) form, matched in simulation to within a few percent at moderate sizes; the exact finite-$n$ constant is open. The qualitative claim — small for all $\zeta$ — is robust.

Corollaries 1 & 3 — meeting the barrier, and complementing aggregation derived

Recovering the wall (Cor. 1): if the attack stops varying, $\Var(a)\to0$, then $T^\star\to\infty$ — undetectable for any number of rounds, and the known floor governs again. The two analyses meet exactly here. Complementarity (Cor. 3): $T^\star$ decreases as the attacker fraction grows — detection is strongest precisely where robust aggregation is weakest. There is no “blinding” as the bad fraction approaches one-half.

Why we needed them: obligation 5 — a new method must be consistent with a proved barrier (reproduce it where it applies), and it is far more useful if it covers the regime the old tool struggles in.

Proposition 6 — it does not rely on a Gaussian attack derived

The earlier statements assumed a Gaussian attack schedule. Removing that: the part that drives detection (the variance along $v$) is schedule-free at leading order; the shape of the schedule (its kurtosis) enters only as a small correction.

$$ \text{rate}\;=\;\frac{T(\omega\beta)^2}{16}\cdot\frac{1}{1+\kappa_p/4}\;+\;O(\beta^3) $$

Why we needed it: real backdoors are not Gaussian. This shows the result does not hinge on that convenience. (Honestly: an intermittent “on/off” attack that hides most rounds is harder than its kurtosis number alone suggests — the test is best-possible only within the Gaussian family, and is merely locally optimal beyond it.)

Proposition 7 — it all runs under privacy proved (idealized primitive)

The whole pipeline runs on group sums alone — the server never sees an individual update — because every step is linear and the spike survives summation. The price is captured by an effective sample count that depends only on the group design $A$:

$$ N_{\mathrm{eff}}(A)=\frac{(\operatorname{tr}M)^2}{\operatorname{tr}(M^2)}\in\Big[\tfrac{n}{s},\,n\Big],\qquad M=A^\top A, \qquad T^\star_{\mathrm{group}}=\frac{d}{N_{\mathrm{eff}}(A)\,\lambda^2}. $$

Why we needed it: obligation 6, and the entire point of the project — detection that respects privacy rather than assuming it away.

Status, split honestly. That $N_{\mathrm{eff}}$ lies between $n/s$ and $n$ is an exact consequence of its definition proved. Its use as the rounds-to-detect predictor is a heuristic, validated in simulation to about 15% empirical. The privacy itself is stated for an idealized additive-masking primitive with a known consensus — the cryptography and a concrete privacy-preserving group code are out of scope here.

9 What it means & what is still open

The takeaway, in one breath: there is a regime — the realistic time-varying coordinated backdoor — where evidence genuinely accumulates across rounds; once the attack direction is recovered, the per-client error bound provably shrinks with more rounds (Proposition 2); and the whole pipeline still runs under privacy. And it relates to the prior barrier in a clean way rather than fighting it:

  • Orthogonal to the barrier. The barrier is gated by heterogeneity; this detector is not, because it reads a variance signal rather than an average one.
  • Complementary to robust aggregation. It is strongest exactly where aggregation is weakest — when the adversarial fraction is large.
  • Gracefully consistent. When the attack goes static, the method reproduces the known floor instead of contradicting it. The two results are different axes that meet at a single point.

What is still open

  • A fully rigorous per-client missed-detection / false-alarm bound (Theorem 1 is so far a derivation by reduction, not a from-scratch proof).
  • The exact constant in $T^\star$, and the precise finite-sample form of the consensus error $\tau^2$.
  • Optimality beyond the Gaussian family (intermittent attacks leave room for a shape-aware test).
  • Instantiating a real privacy-preserving group code that achieves a target privacy level and the overlap needed to localize per client.
  • Detection is conditional by design: it requires a genuinely time-varying attack, a shared direction, and enough rounds. Against a perfect static imitator the method recovers the known floor rather than beating it — and that is the correct behaviour, not a shortcoming.

From my current understanding, this is where the contribution sits: a modest but, I believe, clean result — a probabilistic, across-rounds, per-client detector that holds up under privacy, sidesteps the heterogeneity that stops robust aggregation, and stays honest about the line between what is proved, what is derived, and what is still to do. I would welcome your read on which of the open items is most worth pinning down first.
