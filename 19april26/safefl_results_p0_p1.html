Observation Function Investigation: Why SignGuard Fails, Why Cosine-Median Doesn't

Recap of the factor-graph memory crisis, measurement of the SignGuard observation quality, the mechanism behind its failure, and the cosine-median alternative.

1. Original Design — Persistent Factor Graph OOMs at ~1300 Rounds
Factor count grows with round count; GPU memory does not
v1 broken
Workers are binary latents P(workeri = malicious). Each round's secure-aggregation observation is added to the graph as a k-ary factor over the group's members. The graph is never reset; BP runs over the accumulated structure. At n=500, group_size=10 the graph grows by ~50 factors per round.
factor graph memory persistent
Observed: GPU OOM crash at round ~1304 on CIFAR10/ResNet18 with n=500, f=50, group_size=10, factorGraphs_num_iters=500. Every round's factor-graph JIT-recompile also fragments the JAX XLA allocator, producing a second OOM at round ~26 in some configurations even when total factor count is small.
2. The Redesign — Windowed BP + Seeded Priors
Sliding window of K rounds; older evidence collapsed into per-worker unary priors
v2
Each round the factor graph is rebuilt from scratch. It contains (a) N unary priors seeded from the posterior at round t−K (the prior_snapshot), and (b) K × num_groups k-ary factors from the most recent K rounds of observations. Graph size is constant in time.

v1 — persistent graph

Factors: O(t · num_groups)
Evidence retention: all rounds at full weight
Memory: unbounded
OOM: round ~1304

v2 — windowed + priors

Factors: O(K · num_groups)
Evidence retention: K recent at full weight, older summarized
Memory: bounded (K=10)
OOM: —

K=10 window prior_snapshot O(1) memory/round
Caveat: the memory crisis is solved but the detection quality problem is not — the redesign changed where evidence is kept, not the informativeness of individual observations. Investigating that is what the rest of this deck is about.
3. Yardstick — Youden J in 30 Seconds
J is the gap between flag rate on malicious groups and flag rate on benign groups
Formally J = TPR + TNR − 1 = P(flag | malicious) − P(flag | benign). Range [0, 1] for any informative detector. Three anchor points:
How to read it: if a classifier flags malicious and benign groups at the same rate, it is not using ground truth at all — J = 0. Anything below 0 means it is actively anti-correlated (you would do better by flipping its outputs). The reported J for SignGuard in the next section is +0.008.

5. Why — The Dilution Mechanism at group_size = 10
A single malicious worker is ~10% of its group's gradient sum
At n=500, f=50, malicious fraction is p=0.10. With group_size=10, the probability that a random group contains at least one malicious worker is:
P(≥1 mal) = 1 − (1 − 0.10)10 = 0.651
Two consequences:
  • 65% of groups contain at least one malicious member each round.
  • In every such group, the malicious direction is a ~10% minority of the summed gradient.
The failure in one sentence: SG's KMeans-majority heuristic assumes the mal-containing side is the minority. At group_size × p_mal = 1.0, the mal-containing side is the majority, and the heuristic flags whichever small cluster randomly happens to separate — independent of ground truth.
saturation group_size=10
6. Cosine-Median Observation — Monotonically Improves With Training
The algorithm, one round
Each round has three steps: (1) compute the coordinate-wise median of the 50 bucket-sum gradients to get a single reference vector; (2) score every bucket by cosine similarity to that reference; (3) flag the lower tail of scores using a median − 1.5 · MAD cutoff. Flagged buckets are dropped; the rest are averaged and applied as the global update.
Key property: the reference vector never depends on a clean server dataset — it is derived from the bucket sums themselves. The robust median absorbs mal-contaminated buckets without being pulled off course, because every bucket is >90% benign by construction of group_size=10, p_mal=0.10.
Inspired by:
Grouped by which of our two constraints — bucketed (group-level) inputs and privacy-preserving / secure aggregation — each paper directly addresses.
bucketed inputs  •  privacy: not addressed
  • Karimireddy, He, Jaggi. Byzantine-Robust Learning on Heterogeneous Datasets via Bucketing. ICLR 2022. arxiv.org/abs/2006.09365 — establishes operating on bucketed (aggregated) gradients as the primitive for Byzantine robustness. Bucket size is tuned for variance reduction, not privacy; but the server-sees-only-bucket-means pattern is the same one we rely on.
secure aggregation  •  bucketing: per-worker, not group-level
  • Ma, Ma, Jiang, Liu, Li, Yiu, Luo. ShieldFL: Mitigating Model Poisoning Attacks in Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning. IEEE TIFS 2022. ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9762272 — cosine-similarity filtering under secure aggregation; closest analogue to our privacy constraint. Operates per-worker (not bucketed), uses two-trapdoor homomorphic encryption to compute cosines without exposing individual gradients.
foundational primitives  •  per-worker, no privacy layer
  • Yin, Chen, Kannan, Bartlett. Byzantine-Robust Distributed Learning: Towards Optimal Statistical Rates. ICML 2018. arxiv.org/abs/1803.01498 — coordinate-wise median / trimmed mean as robust aggregators. Source of the median primitive itself.
  • Cao, Fang, Liu, Gong. FLTrust: Byzantine-robust Federated Learning via Trust Bootstrapping. NDSS 2021. arxiv.org/abs/2012.13995 — cosine similarity of client updates against a reference direction. Uses a clean server dataset (which we do not have); motivated the cosine-to-reference primitive we replaced with a data-derived median reference.
  • Karimireddy, He, Jaggi. Learning from History for Byzantine Robust Optimization. ICML 2021. arxiv.org/abs/2012.10333 — CCLIP: using an aggregated-reference direction as the clipping anchor for robust optimization.

Why it improves with rounds: benign direction stabilizes while the attack does not
The detector is a direction-based comparison. As training progresses the benign loss surface flattens near a minimum, so benign gradient norms shrink and their directions stabilize. Malicious (label-flipped) gradients don't share the same convergence — they keep a consistent "wrong" direction with bounded norm. The cosine gap between mal-containing group sums and the coordinate-wise median therefore widens over time.

7. Future Directions — Backdoor and the Role of the Factor Graph
Which detector works for which attack
hypothesis
Measurements in Sections 3–6 cover label_flipping_attack only. Other categories behave differently by construction:
Label Flipping — direction attack Backdoor — magnitude attack

SignGuard (norm + sign filter) J = +0.008   (measured) designed for this   (hyp.)
Literature: Karimireddy et al. ICLR 2022 evaluates BF, LF, Mimic, IPM, ALIE — no backdoor / targeted attack.
“However, this is a challenging and open problem (Sun et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020).” — Karimireddy, He, Jaggi, ICLR 2022, Section 2
Research paths
open

A — reconcile

Ensemble cos_median + SG, BP integrates both as parallel factors. Unified defense across direction and magnitude attacks.

Stronger claim. Harder to validate.

B — narrow to backdoor

Frame paper as “Byzantine-robust FL under SA for backdoor.” The gap spans both primary lineages of prior work, neither of which evaluates backdoor:

  • bucketing — Karimireddy 2022 (see quote above): backdoor is an explicit scope exclusion.
  • privacy / SA — ShieldFL, BREA, ByzSecAgg, RFLPA: focus on model-poisoning attacks; targeted / backdoor under SA is uncharacterized.

Limitations to own:

  • no unified detector — attack category picks the observation function.
  • detection quality is training-phase dependent (J starts near 0).
  • backdoor claim is hypothesis; gating experiment below.

Cleaner. Narrower.

C — ease constraints

Relax a problem parameter to simplify detection. Two knobs — both have costs:

  • lower nbyz — LF barely degrades FedAvg without any defense at low attack rates; removes the motivation for the defense itself.
  • smaller group_size — restores per-group signal, but group_size=10 is already thin anonymity for 500 workers; smaller raises collusion risk.
Gating experiment for A vs B: SG vs cos_median on backdoor (same config, swap byz_type). Decides whether A is feasible (both detectors contribute) or B is forced (one dominates).
