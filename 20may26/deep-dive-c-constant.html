Scale equivariant · Stateless

Overview and where we fit

Overview
Lower bound (Theorem 3) Proves $\Omega(\delta\rho^2)$ is unavoidable via indistinguishability. This establishes $\delta\rho^2$ as the natural scale.
Definition 1 (the abstraction) Defines $(\delta_{\max}, c)$-ARAgg with error $\le c\delta\rho^2$. This is the unique form matching the lower bound. iWhat I'd need to show (step 1): that my aggregator is scale-equivariant, i.e. $A(\alpha x_1, \dots, \alpha x_n) = \alpha \cdot A(x_1, \dots, x_n)$. This forces error to be linear in $\rho^2$ via $h(st) = s \cdot h(t)$. A Bayesian-weighted aggregator should satisfy this since the belief vector $\pi$ is dimensionless (probabilities, not gradients).
Bucketing lemma (Lemma C.1) Proves $\tilde\rho^2 = \rho^2/s$ and $\tilde\delta = \delta s$, giving invariant $\tilde\delta \cdot \tilde\rho^2 = \delta\rho^2$.
Aggregator-specific proofs (Theorem 2) Each aggregator proves its own $f(\delta_{\max})$. Combined with bucketing, this becomes $c = f(\delta_{\max})/\delta_{\max}$. iWhat I'd need to show (step 2): a bound $\E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2 \le f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \tilde\rho^2$ for my specific aggregator. $f(\delta_{\max})$ measures degradation near the breakdown point, then $c = f(\delta_{\max}) / \delta_{\max}$. For a Bayesian aggregator, $f$ would depend on how concentrated $\pi$ is. Worst case (Mimic, $\pi$ stays flat): same $f$ as stateless.
Convergence (Theorem 2, general) Any $(\delta_{\max}, c)$-ARAgg plugs into the optimization bound: error floor $\sim c\delta\zeta^2$.
The one sentence summary

Each aggregator pays its own constant $c$ for imperfect filtering. The $\delta\rho^2$ scaling it multiplies is universal: scale equivariance forces error $\propto \rho^2$, the bucket size constraint $s = \delta_{\max}/\delta$ introduces $\delta$, and the lower bound proves $\Omega(\delta\rho^2)$ is tight.

1. The definition: what $c$ measures

Definition 1 · main.tex:329

$(\delta_{\max}, c)$-Agnostic Robust Aggregator

Given inputs $\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ of which a good subset $\mathcal{G}$ of size $|\mathcal{G}| > (1-\delta)n$ for $\delta \le \delta_{\max} < 0.5$ satisfies $\E\|x_i - x_j\|^2 \le \rho^2$, the output $\hat{x}$ satisfies:

$$\E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2 \;\le\; \cb{c}\;\co{\delta}\;\cg{\rho^2}$$

where $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{G}|}\sum_{j \in \mathcal{G}} x_j$. The aggregator does not need to know $\rho^2$.

What each piece means

$\co{\delta}$ — Byzantine fraction (their voting power).

$\cg{\rho^2}$ — good-input spread (the cover attackers hide within).

$\cb{c}$ — aggregator-specific filtering cost. A perfect aggregator has $c = 1$; real ones pay more.

$c$ is not assumed. It is derived from each aggregator's proof. All aggregators, combined with bucketing, land on $O(\delta\rho^2)$. The next section shows why the $\rho^2$ part is forced.

2. Why the error is linear in $\rho^2$: scale equivariance

The form $f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \rho^2$ is forced by a symmetry all agnostic aggregators share. Scale every input by $\alpha$:

Effect of scaling inputs by $\alpha$
$\bar{x} \to \alpha\bar{x}$ Mean scales. The average of scaled vectors is the scaled average.
$\rho^2 \to \alpha^2\rho^2$ Variance scales quadratically. $\E\|\alpha x_i - \alpha x_j\|^2 = \alpha^2 \E\|x_i - x_j\|^2$.
$\hat{x} \to \alpha\hat{x}$ Output scales (if the aggregator is scale-equivariant).
$\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2 \to \alpha^2\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2$ Error scales quadratically, matching $\rho^2$.

Which aggregators are scale-equivariant?

$A$ is scale-equivariant if $A(\alpha x_1, \ldots, \alpha x_n) = \alpha \cdot A(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$:

Aggregator Scale equivariant? Why
Krum Yes Minimizes $\sum\|x_i - x_j\|^2$ over neighbors. Scaling multiplies all squared distances by $\alpha^2$; argmin unchanged, selected point scales by $\alpha$.
Geometric median Yes Minimizes $\sum\|y - y_i\|$. The minimizer of the scaled problem is $\alpha$ times the original minimizer.
Coordinate-wise median Yes $\operatorname{median}(\alpha x_i^{(j)}) = \alpha \cdot \operatorname{median}(x_i^{(j)})$ for each coordinate.
CClip No Clipping radius $\tau$ is fixed. When inputs scale by $\alpha$, $\tau$ stays put, so clipping behavior changes. Not agnostic.

The functional equation

Derivation

Scale equivariance forces linearity in $\rho^2$

Let $h(\rho^2, \delta_{\max}) := \E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2$. After scaling all inputs by $\alpha$:

  • New variance: $\alpha^2\rho^2$
  • New error: $\alpha^2 \cdot h(\rho^2, \delta_{\max})$ (by scale equivariance)
  • Byzantine fraction $\delta$: unchanged (scaling doesn't change who is Byzantine)

Since $\delta_{\max}$ is unaffected by scaling, the new error also equals $h(\alpha^2\rho^2, \delta_{\max})$:

$$h(\alpha^2\rho^2,\; \delta_{\max}) \;=\; \alpha^2 \cdot h(\rho^2,\; \delta_{\max}) \qquad \forall\;\alpha > 0$$

Setting $t = \rho^2$ and $s = \alpha^2$, this gives $h(st) = s \cdot h(t)$ for all $s > 0$, $t > 0$.
Why $\alpha > 0$? We need $s = \alpha^2 > 0$ since it represents a variance ratio. $\alpha^2 > 0$ for any $\alpha \ne 0$, so the sign of $\alpha$ doesn't matter. Writing $\alpha > 0$ just ensures $s$ sweeps all positive reals.

This must hold for every positive $s$ and $t$, so we can pick $t = 1$:

Step A. Start with the general rule, valid for all $s > 0$ and all $t > 0$:

$$h(s \cdot t) = s \cdot h(t)$$

Step B. Pick $t = 1$. LHS becomes $h(s \cdot 1) = h(s)$, RHS becomes $s \cdot h(1)$:

$$h(s) = s \cdot h(1)$$

$h(s \cdot 1)$ is just $h(s)$. The two-variable equation collapses: $h$ at any argument $s$ equals $s$ times the constant $h(1)$.

Step C. This holds for every $s > 0$, so $h$ is linear through the origin with slope $h(1)$.

Step D. Substitute $s = \rho^2$:

$$h(\rho^2) = \rho^2 \cdot h(1) = \underbrace{h(1, \delta_{\max})}_{= f(\delta_{\max})} \cdot\; \rho^2$$

$\alpha$ was a probe to discover $h$'s form. Once we know $h$ is linear, $\alpha$ has done its job.

Limitation

Scaling only probes $h$'s dependence on $\rho^2$. It says nothing about $\delta$ (scaling doesn't change who is Byzantine). So we get $h(\rho^2, \delta_{\max}) = f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \rho^2$, but $f(\delta_{\max})$ could be anything. The form of $f$ requires the actual aggregator-specific proofs from the appendix.

Agnosticism = scale equivariance

The definition requires the aggregator to work without knowing $\rho^2$. Scale equivariance is why it doesn't need to: $\rho^2$ factors out cleanly. CClip fails this because its clipping radius $\tau$ is absolute, so its quality depends on $\tau/\rho$.

Why $\rho^2$ and not $\rho$ or $\rho^4$?

Both error $\E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2$ and variance $\rho^2 = \E\|x_i - x_j\|^2$ are squared norms, both scaling as $\alpha^2$. They match. $\rho^4$ would scale as $\alpha^4$, breaking the relationship.

3. Breakdown point vs. $\delta_{\max}$ vs. $\delta$

The paper uses $\delta_{\max}$ in ways that confused me at first. There are three distinct quantities:

The three-level hierarchy
breakdown point The fraction where the proof denominator hits zero. Fixed by the algorithm: $\frac{1}{4}$ for Krum, $\frac{1}{2}$ for GM/CM.
$\delta_{\max}$ Chosen strictly below breakdown: $\delta_{\max} = \text{breakdown} - \nu$, where $\nu > 0$ is the safety margin.
$\delta$ Actual fraction of bad workers. Must satisfy $\delta \le \delta_{\max}$.
Hierarchy

The ordering that must always hold

$$\co{\delta} \;\le\; \co{\delta_{\max}} \;=\; \cr{\text{breakdown}} - \cp{\nu} \;<\; \cr{\text{breakdown}}$$
Analogy

Bridge rated for 10 tons ($\cr{\text{breakdown}}$), policy set to $10 - \nu$ tons ($\co{\delta_{\max}}$), today's truck weighs $\co{\delta}$ tons. $\cp{\nu}$ keeps $c$ finite.

4. Concrete example first: geometric median

GM is the cleanest to trace end-to-end: four steps, no tricks.

4a. The GM proof: deriving $f(\delta_{\max})$

GM operates on bucketed inputs $\{y_1, \ldots, y_m\}$ with variance $\tilde\rho^2$ and Byzantine fraction $\tilde\delta \le \delta_{\max} = \frac{1}{2} - \nu$.

Deriving $f(\delta_{\max})$ for geometric median appendix.tex:1042–1067
Triangle inequality on good and bad buckets

The geometric median $y^\star$ minimizes $\sum_i \|y - y_i\|$. For good $i$ and bad $j$:

$$\|y^\star - y_i\| \ge \|y^\star - \bar{x}\| - \|y_i - \bar{x}\| \qquad (i \in \tilde{\mathcal{G}})$$ $$\|y^\star - y_j\| \ge \|y_j - \bar{x}\| - \|y^\star - \bar{x}\| \qquad\; (j \in \tilde{\mathcal{B}})$$
Bounds the median's distance from the true mean using distances from good and bad buckets.
Sum over all buckets, use optimality

Summing and using the fact that $y^\star$ minimizes the total distance:

$$\|y^\star - \bar{x}\| \le \frac{2}{|\tilde{\mathcal{G}}| - |\tilde{\mathcal{B}}|} \sum_{i \in \tilde{\mathcal{G}}} \|y_i - \bar{x}\|$$
Denominator $|\tilde{\mathcal{G}}| - |\tilde{\mathcal{B}}|$ is the majority margin. Good buckets must outnumber bad.
Square, apply Cauchy-Schwarz, take expectation
$$\E\|y^\star - \bar{x}\|^2 \le \frac{4|\tilde{\mathcal{G}}|^2}{(m - 2|\tilde{\mathcal{B}}|)^2} \cdot \tilde\rho^2$$
Cauchy-Schwarz: $(\sum \|y_i - \bar{x}\|)^2 \le |\tilde{\mathcal{G}}| \cdot \sum \|y_i - \bar{x}\|^2$.
Substitute $|\tilde{\mathcal{B}}| \le m(\frac{1}{2} - \nu)$
$$\E\|y^\star - \bar{x}\|^2 \le \frac{4m^2}{(2m\nu)^2}\tilde\rho^2 = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\nu^2}}_{f(\delta_{\max})} \cdot\; \tilde\rho^2$$
$\nu$ is the gap between Byzantine fraction and breakdown point. As $\nu \to 0$, $f(\delta_{\max}) \to \infty$.
■
Three things to note

1. $\nu$ is the safety margin from §3. Smaller gap = bigger $f(\delta_{\max})$.

2. The result is $f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \tilde\rho^2$, not $c \cdot \delta \cdot \rho^2$ yet. The proof only sees bucketed inputs.

3. The bound is proportional to $\tilde\rho^2$ because GM is scale-equivariant (§2).

4b. The bridge: from $f(\delta_{\max})$ to $c$

Now substitute what bucketing produces:

The bridge

Bucketing substitution turns $f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \tilde\rho^2$ into $c \cdot \delta\rho^2$

From the GM proof:

$$\E\|y^\star - \bar{x}\|^2 \;\le\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{\nu^2}}_{f(\delta_{\max})} \cdot\; \tilde\rho^2$$

From the bucketing lemma (with $s = \lfloor\delta_{\max}/\delta\rfloor$):

$$\tilde\rho^2 = \frac{\rho^2}{s} = \frac{\delta\rho^2}{\delta_{\max}} = \frac{\delta\rho^2}{1/2 - \nu}$$

Substitute:

$$\frac{1}{\nu^2} \;\cdot\; \frac{\delta\rho^2}{1/2 - \nu} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{\nu^2(1/2 - \nu)}}_{c} \;\cdot\; \delta\rho^2$$
Summary

$f(\delta_{\max}) = \frac{1}{\nu^2}$ absorbed the $\delta_{\max}$ denominator from $\tilde\rho^2 = \delta\rho^2/\delta_{\max}$, giving $c = \frac{1}{\nu^2(1/2 - \nu)}$. The $\delta\rho^2$ factor came from bucketing. Same pattern for every aggregator; only $f(\delta_{\max})$ changes.

§5 shows why bucketing always preserves $\delta\rho^2$, §6 generalizes to any aggregator, §7 shows $\delta\rho^2$ is tight.

5. The bucketing invariant: why $\delta\rho^2$ is preserved

The bucketing lemma (Lemma C.1) does two things simultaneously:

Lemma C.1 · appendix.tex:897

Bucketing reduces variance, amplifies Byzantine fraction

Before bucketing:

  • Good-pair variance: $\rho^2$
  • Byzantine fraction: $\delta$

After $s$-bucketing into $m = \lceil n/s \rceil$ buckets:

$$\underbrace{\E\|y_i - y_j\|^2 \le \frac{\rho^2}{s}}_{\text{variance shrinks}} \qquad\qquad \underbrace{\tilde\delta = \delta s}_{\text{Byzantine fraction grows}}$$
The cancellation

$\tilde\delta \times \tilde\rho^2 = (\delta s) \times \frac{\rho^2}{s} = \delta\rho^2$. The product is invariant under bucketing regardless of $s$.

The product doesn't change, but bucketing reshapes the problem:

What bucketing does
Before Low $\delta$ + high $\rho^2$. Good inputs spread out, aggregator confuses heterogeneity with attacks.
After Higher $\tilde\delta$ + lower $\tilde\rho^2$. Good bucket-averages cluster tightly, easier to separate from Byzantines.

Bucketing puts the aggregator back in the low-variance regime it was designed for.

6. Why every aggregator achieves $O(\delta\rho^2)$

The $\delta\rho^2$ scaling is not a coincidence. Any base aggregator combined with bucketing gives $O(\delta\rho^2)$:

General argument: bucketing + any base aggregator → $O(\delta\rho^2)$ structural, not aggregator-specific
Start with any base aggregator $A$

Suppose $A$ can handle up to $\delta_{\max}$ fraction of Byzantines. On inputs with good-pair variance $\rho^2$, it achieves:

$$\E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2 \le f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \rho^2$$

where $f(\delta_{\max})$ is some function specific to $A$.

$f$ is aggregator-specific (Krum: $\frac{4}{3\nu}$, GM: $\frac{1}{\nu^2}$) but always a function of $\delta_{\max}$ only.
Apply bucketing with $s = \lfloor\delta_{\max}/\delta\rfloor$

By the bucketing lemma, the post-bucketing quantities are:

$$\tilde\rho^2 = \frac{\rho^2}{s} = \frac{\rho^2 \cdot \delta}{\delta_{\max}} \qquad\qquad \tilde\delta = \delta s \approx \delta_{\max}$$
Bucket size chosen so $\tilde\delta \approx \delta_{\max}$, right at the aggregator's tolerance limit.
Feed bucketed inputs into $A$

The aggregator sees inputs with variance $\tilde\rho^2$ and Byzantine fraction $\tilde\delta \approx \delta_{\max}$. Applying its guarantee:

$$\E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2 \le f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \tilde\rho^2 = f(\delta_{\max}) \cdot \frac{\delta\rho^2}{\delta_{\max}}$$
Both $f(\delta_{\max})$ and $\delta_{\max}$ are constants, so:
Identify $c$
$$\E\|\hat{x} - \bar{x}\|^2 \le \underbrace{\frac{f(\delta_{\max})}{\delta_{\max}}}_{= c \text{ (a constant)}} \cdot\; \delta\rho^2$$
$c$ absorbs aggregator-specific details. $\delta\rho^2$ comes from bucketing's $\tilde\rho^2 = \rho^2 \cdot \delta/\delta_{\max}$.
■
Takeaway

$\delta\rho^2$ is universal because it comes from bucketing, which sits in front of every aggregator. The aggregator only determines $f(\delta_{\max})$, which collapses into $c$.

7. The lower bound: $\delta\rho^2$ is the best any aggregator can do

Theorem 3 · main.tex:447 · appendix.tex:1100–1153

Information-theoretic lower bound

No algorithm can do better than $\Omega(\delta\rho^2)$. For any algorithm $\mathcal{A}$, there exist inputs where:

$$\E\|\nabla f(\mathcal{A}(\ldots))\|^2 \;\ge\; \Omega(\delta\zeta^2)$$

The proof constructs two scenarios with identical observations but different optima. The algorithm must be wrong on at least one, giving $\Omega(\delta\zeta^2)$ error. This matches the upper bound from §6, so $\delta\rho^2$ is tight up to $c$.
